Key Concepts: Privacy by Design & GAPP/GDPR Alignment

Privacy by Design (PbD): The 7 Principles

1. Proactive not Reactive

Anticipate and prevent privacy-invasive events before they happen, rather than waiting for breaches to occur.

2. Privacy as the Default

Ensure personal data is automatically protected in any system. No action is required by the user to protect their privacy—it's built-in.

3. Privacy Embedded into Design

Embed privacy directly into the design and architecture of IT systems and business practices, making it a core component.

4. Full Functionality (Positive-Sum)

Accommodate all legitimate interests in a "win-win" manner. Avoid the false, zero-sum trade-off between privacy and functionality.

5. End-to-End Security

Apply security measures from start to finish, protecting data throughout its entire lifecycle—from collection to secure destruction.

6. Visibility and Transparency

Keep operations and practices transparent. Inform users about what data is collected, why, and how it's processed, and ensure compliance is verifiable.

7. Respect for User Privacy

Keep the individual's interests at the forefront. Offer strong privacy defaults, appropriate notice, and user-friendly options (e.g., access, correction).

The Fourth Amendment & Evolving Privacy

The word "privacy" does not appear in the U.S. Constitution. Instead, privacy rights are derived from several amendments, most notably the Fourth Amendment's protection against "unreasonable searches and seizures." The Supreme Court's interpretation of this right has evolved significantly with technology.

Olmstead v. United States (1928)

The "Trespass Doctrine." The Court ruled that wiretapping a phone line from outside the home was not a search. Why? There was no physical trespass onto the person's property. This tied the Fourth Amendment's protection to physical intrusion.

Katz v. United States (11967)

"Reasonable Expectation of Privacy." This landmark case overturned *Olmstead*. The Court ruled that placing a listening device on a public phone booth was a search. Justice Harlan's concurrence established the two-part test: 1) Did the person have a subjective expectation of privacy? 2) Is that expectation one that society recognizes as "reasonable"?

Carpenter v. United States (2018)

Digital Data & The Third-Party Doctrine. The Court ruled that accessing 7+ days of historical cell-site location information (CSLI) is a search requiring a warrant. This limited the "third-party doctrine" (which normally says you have no privacy in data you give to a third party, like a bank) for new, pervasive digital data.

GAPP & GDPR: Principles in Parallel

The 10 GAPP (Generally Accepted Privacy Principles) from AICPA/CICA provide a comprehensive framework that shows significant overlap with the 7 core processing principles from GDPR Article 5.

1. Management

Defines, documents, communicates, and assigns accountability for privacy policies and procedures.

Aligns with GDPR: Accountability

2. Notice

Provides notice about its privacy policies and procedures and identifies the purposes for which personal information is collected, used, retained, and disclosed.

Aligns with GDPR: Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency

3. Choice and Consent

Describes the choices available to the individual and obtains implicit or explicit consent with respect to the collection, use, and disclosure of personal information.

Aligns with GDPR: Lawfulness (Consent as legal basis)

4. Collection

Collects personal information only for the purposes identified in the notice.

Aligns with GDPR: Purpose Limitation Aligns with GDPR: Data Minimization

5. Use, Retention, and Disposal

Limits the use of personal information to the purposes identified in the notice and for which the individual has provided consent. Retains for only as long as necessary and disposes of it securely.

Aligns with GDPR: Purpose Limitation Aligns with GDPR: Storage Limitation

6. Access

Provides individuals with access to their personal information for review and correction.

Aligns with GDPR: Accuracy (as a Data Subject Right)

7. Disclosure to Third Parties

Discloses personal information to third parties only for the purposes identified in the notice and with the individual's consent.

Aligns with GDPR: Lawfulness & Transparency

8. Security for Privacy

Protects personal information against unauthorized access (both physical and logical) using appropriate security measures.

Aligns with GDPR: Integrity & Confidentiality

9. Quality

Maintains accurate, complete, and relevant personal information for the purposes identified in the notice.

Aligns with GDPR: Accuracy

10. Monitoring and Enforcement

Monitors compliance with its privacy policies and procedures and has procedures to address complaints and disputes.

Aligns with GDPR: Accountability

Key ISO Standards for Privacy Management

ISO standards provide internationally recognized frameworks for managing information security and privacy. `ISO/IEC 27701` is the key standard for mapping privacy controls to global regulations.

ISO/IEC 27701: The Privacy Extension

This standard provides a Privacy Information Management System (PIMS). It's not a standalone certification; it's an extension to the popular ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management System).

ISO/IEC 27001 (ISMS Foundation)
+
ISO/IEC 27701 (PIMS Extension)
=
Demonstrable Privacy (Maps to GDPR, CCPA)

How ISO 27701 Maps to Laws

The standard's annexes provide direct mapping tables showing how its controls help fulfill the requirements of major privacy laws.

ISO 27701 Control (Example)

"6.3.1.1: Identify and document the purpose for processing PII."

Maps to GDPR: Art. 5(1)(b) (Purpose Limitation)
ISO 27701 Control (Example)

"6.4.2.1: Implement mechanisms for data subjects to access, correct, or erase their PII."

Maps to GDPR: Art. 15, 16, 17 (Rights) Maps to CCPA: § 1798.100, .105 (Rights)
ISO 27701 Control (Example)

"7.2.5: Implement policies for Privacy by Design and by Default."

Maps to GDPR: Art. 25 (PbD/PbD)
