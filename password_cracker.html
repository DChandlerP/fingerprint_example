Methodology & Sources

The calculations above assume a "Fast Hash" scenario, specifically NTLM (common in Windows environments). This represents a "worst-case" for defenders, as these hashes are computed extremely quickly by modern GPUs.

  • Gaming PC (RTX 4090): Approx 300 GH/s on NTLM. Source: Hashcat Benchmarks.
  • Botnet: Estimated aggregate power of 10,000 compromised consumer PCs.
  • NSA Array: A hypothetical supercomputer facility dedicated to cracking.

Note: Stronger hashing algorithms (like bcrypt, Argon2, or PBKDF2) are orders of magnitude slower to crack. We show NTLM to demonstrate why password length is critical even against fast attacks.

Why Passphrases?

For years, we were taught to use "Complex" passwords like Tr0ub4dor&3. The problem? Computers are great at guessing these substitutions, but humans are bad at remembering them.

Tr0ub4dor&3 ~28 bits (Weak)
correct horse battery staple ~44 bits (Strong)

The Math of Length: Entropy ($E$) comes from the pool of characters ($N$) and length ($L$): $E = L \times \log_2(N)$. Increasing length ($L$) has a massive exponential effect. Adding just one word to a passphrase adds far more safety than adding a symbol to a short password.

This "Passphrase" strategy is now recommended by NIST SP 800-63B and popularized by XKCD 936.
